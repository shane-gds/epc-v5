How you "teach" Splink:

You tell Splink how to treat a column by mapping it to a Comparison Template in your settings dictionary.

In our code, we wrote:

cl.NameComparison("first_name")

Behind the scenes, this tells Splink:

    "Hey, look at the column called first_name. I want you to treat it like a human name. This means you should check for exact matches, but also calculate fuzzy matches (like Jaro-Winkler distance) because humans typo names constantly."

If you had an NHS number, you wouldn't use NameComparison. You'd use cl.ExactMatch("nhs_number") because there is no such thing as a "nickname" for an NHS number!

You are the brain; Splink is just the muscle executing the rules you configure.
📊 Question 2: How does Splink know "John" is common and "Bort" is rare?

This is where the math gets incredibly elegant. Splink doesn't come pre-programmed with a giant dictionary of baby names. It figures out that "John" is common and "Bort" is rare entirely by looking at your actual input dataset.

This is done using a feature called Term Frequency Adjustments (TF Adjustments).

Here is how Splink does it in three steps:
Step 1: The "Roll Call" (Counting)

Before it does any matching, Splink runs a quick SQL query over your dataset to count how often every single value appears.

    It looks at your data and says: "Okay, I see the name 'John' appears 5,000 times. But I only see the name 'Bort' appears 1 time."

Step 2: Calculating Coincidence

Splink uses these counts to adjust the $u$-probability (the "coincidence factor" we talked about in Lesson 3) dynamically for each specific name.

    For "John": If you pull two random people out of your dataset, the probability that they are both named "John" by pure coincidence is quite high (because there are 5,000 of them).
    For "Bort": If you pull two random people out of your dataset, the probability that they are both named "Bort" by pure coincidence is practically zero (because there is only one "Bort" in the entire system!).

Step 3: Giving Out the Points (The Scale Tips)

Now, when Splink is comparing two records:

    The "John" Match: * "Record A is named John, and Record B is named John."
        Splink says: "Meh. A match, but since 'John' is super common in this dataset, it might just be a coincidence. +1.5 points."
    The "Bort" Match:
        "Record A is named Bort, and Record B is named Bort."
        Splink says: "Holy cow! What are the odds of two different people being named 'Bort'? Almost zero! This is a massive clue. +8.0 points!"

🧠 ADHD Brain Cheat-Sheet Summary

    How does it know what columns are? It doesn't. You tell it what they are using comparison templates in your python configuration.
    How does it know "John" vs "Bort"? It counts them in your data first.
        Lots of matches on a specific value = Splink lowers the match points (low uniqueness).
        Rare matches on a specific value = Splink jacks up the match points (high uniqueness).

By calculating these "Term Frequency" adjustments, Splink makes your model incredibly precise without you ever having to manually tell it which names are common in the real world!

Once both of your datasets have latitude and longitude columns, you can use one of Splink's most powerful generic comparison templates: cl.DistanceInKMAtThresholds.

settings = {
    "link_type": "link_only",
    "blocking_rules_to_generate_predictions": [

        cl.ExactMatch("house_number"),
        cl.NameComparison("street_name"),

        cl.DistanceInKMAtThresholds(

            "longitude",

Option A: The Fast Mathematical Way (Haversine)

    target.uprn,
    target.address1,
    target.postcode,
    target.current_energy_efficiency,
    -- Earth radius = 6371km. This calculates straight-line distance.
    (6371 * acos(
        cos(radians(source.lat)) * cos(radians(target.lat)) * cos(radians(target.long) - radians(source.long)) +
        sin(radians(source.lat)) * sin(radians(target.lat))
    )) AS distance_km
FROM my_linked_properties source
CROSS JOIN my_linked_properties target
WHERE source.uprn = '100023345678' -- Your starting property
  AND target.wall_insulation_recommendation = 'YES' -- Needs insulation
  AND target.current_energy_efficiency IN ('D', 'E', 'F', 'G') -- EPC Band below C
  AND target.uprn != source.uprn -- Don't compare to itself
  -- Filter distance within 1km
  AND (6371 * acos(
        cos(radians(source.lat)) * cos(radians(target.lat)) * cos(radians(target.long) - radians(source.long)) +
        sin(radians(source.lat)) * sin(radians(target.lat))
  )) <= 1.0
ORDER BY distance_km ASC;
